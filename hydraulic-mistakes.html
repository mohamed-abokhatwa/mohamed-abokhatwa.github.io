Common Mistakes in Hydraulic Modeling of Water Transmission Networks

By Mohamed Abokhatwa  ·  Technical Manager | PMP, PMI-RMP, ENV SP  ·  May 2026

Introduction

After two decades of designing and managing water transmission networks — from 400 km+ pipeline systems in Waad Al-Shamal to 80 km transmission networks for NEOM — I've reviewed dozens of hydraulic models. One truth remains constant: the model is only as reliable as the assumptions behind it.

1

Ignoring Transient (Surge) Analysis Entirely

Many design teams model only steady-state conditions. In a transmission pipeline designed for 145,000 m3/day at 8 bar steady-state, a sudden pump trip can generate a pressure surge exceeding 20-25 bar within 2-3 seconds — more than 3x the design operating pressure.

Design Rule: For pipelines longer than 2 km or pump heads exceeding 30 m, transient analysis is mandatory.
2

Incorrect Wave Speed Calculation

The pressure surge depends on wave speed (a) defined by the Joukowsky equation: DP = r.a.DV

Pipe MaterialWave Speed (a)
Ductile Iron (DI)~1,000-1,100 m/s
Carbon Steel (CS)~1,200-1,400 m/s
HDPE~200-400 m/s
GRP/FRP~400-700 m/s
3

Neglecting Pipe Profile and Topography

In projects like Al-Baha, elevation changes of 200-400 m over 10-30 km are common. When pressure drops below vapor pressure, column separation occurs — a vapor cavity forms then collapses violently.

Always superimpose the transient pressure envelope on the pipe profile.
4

Improper Air Valve Sizing and Placement

4a — Air valves needed at all local high points, not just major ones.

4b — For DN 1,200 mm DI at 2.0 m/s: flow = 8,140 m3/h, required air inflow = 1,800 Nm3/h — frequently underestimated.

4c — Slam effect: slow-closing or anti-slam air valves must be specified.

5

Surge Protection Devices Specified Without Simulation

6

Using Steady-State Model for Emergency Scenarios

For demand changes occurring over less than 30-60 seconds, transient analysis is required — not steady-state.
