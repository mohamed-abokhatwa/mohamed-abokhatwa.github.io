Sizing the Hydropneumatic Surge Vessel in Water Transmission Pipelines

By Mohamed Abokhatwa  ·  Technical Manager | PMP, PMI-RMP, ENV SP  ·  May 2026

Introduction

The hydropneumatic surge vessel is one of the most effective devices for mitigating water hammer in water transmission pipelines. It acts as a local pressure reservoir — supplying water when pressure drops after a pump trip, and absorbing excess water when pressure rises.

Despite its critical role, surge vessel sizing is frequently oversimplified or based on rules of thumb disconnected from actual system behaviour. In this article, I present a structured design workflow — from defining system inputs to interpreting Bentley HAMMER simulation outputs — using a real-scale DN 800 mm pipeline example. The goal is to show not just the what, but the how of surge vessel sizing in practice.

1. How the Surge Vessel Works

The surge vessel is a closed pressure vessel, partially filled with water and partially with compressed gas (nitrogen). It is connected directly to the pipeline at or near the pump station discharge. During normal operation, the vessel is pressurised to match the steady-state pipeline pressure. When a pump trip occurs, two phases follow:

  • Phase 1 — Down-surge: Pipeline pressure drops. The vessel expels water into the pipeline to prevent column separation and sub-atmospheric pressures.
  • Phase 2 — Up-surge: The returning pressure wave compresses the gas cushion, absorbing the excess pressure rise and preventing over-pressurisation.
The vessel does not eliminate the transient — it modulates it. The gas cushion acts as a spring: the larger and softer the spring, the gentler the modulation. But an oversized vessel adds unnecessary cost, while an undersized one fails to protect the system.

2. The Governing Equation — Polytropic Gas Law

The behaviour of the gas cushion in the vessel is governed by the polytropic gas law:

P0 · V0^n = P1 · V1^n = P2 · V2^n = constant
P0, V0 = initial steady-state gas pressure and volume  |  P1, V1 = state at minimum pressure (down-surge)  |  P2, V2 = state at maximum pressure (up-surge)  |  n = polytropic exponent

In practice, n = 1.2 is commonly adopted as a conservative intermediate value — between isothermal (n = 1.0, slow events) and adiabatic (n = 1.4, fast events). This is the standard adopted for water transmission surge vessel sizing (Chaudhry, 2014; Thorley, 2004).

The volume of water exchanged between the vessel and the pipeline during the transient is:

Water expelled = V1 − V0   |   Water absorbed = V0 − V2
These two quantities define the minimum required vessel working volume for down-surge and up-surge protection respectively.

5. Surge Vessel Sizing — Step-by-Step Procedure

1Define steady-state operating conditions

From the steady-state model: P0 = 85.0 m + 10.3 m atmospheric = 95.3 m abs = 1.84 bar abs at the vessel tap point. This is the pre-charge pressure — it must reflect the actual HGL at the vessel connection, not the pump discharge head.

2Define allowable pressure limits

  • P_max = 136 m (PN 16 rating minus 15% safety margin)
  • P_min = +3.0 m gauge (above vapour pressure with safety margin, confirmed with client)

3Set the initial gas volume

The initial gas volume occupies a fraction of the total vessel volume. A common starting ratio is 50% gas / 50% water at steady state. This ratio directly controls how much water the vessel can expel (gas expands during down-surge) and absorb (gas compresses during up-surge).

4Analytical starting point — polytropic gas law

Assuming initial gas volume V0 = 3.0 m³ (50% of an estimated 6.0 m³ vessel):

V1 = V0 × (P0/P1)^(1/n) = 3.0 × (1.84/1.30)^(1/1.2) = 3.0 × 1.337 = 4.01 m³
Water to be expelled = V1 − V0 = 4.01 − 3.0 = 1.01 m³  |  Initial vessel estimate: 6.0 m³

5Simulation iteration results

Three vessel volumes were tested in Bentley HAMMER. For each run, the pre-charge pressure was set to 1.84 bar abs with a 50/50 gas-to-water ratio at steady state.

7. Critical Design Parameters Often Overlooked

7.1 Pre-charge pressure

The initial gas pressure must match the steady-state HGL at the vessel connection point — not the pump head, not the system average. On a long pipeline with significant friction losses, these can differ by 10–30 m — a critical error in the sizing calculation.

Setting P0 based on pump discharge head instead of the actual HGL at the vessel tap point is one of the most common — and most consequential — mistakes in surge vessel design.

7.2 Connection pipe diameter

The pipe connecting the vessel to the main pipeline must allow rapid water exchange during the transient. An undersized connection pipe creates a hydraulic throttle: the vessel cannot respond fast enough, and the transient is not effectively modulated.

D_connection ≥ 0.3 × D_main
For DN 800 mm main pipeline: connection pipe minimum DN 250 mm

7.3 Asymmetric throttle orifice

In many designs, a throttle orifice is installed on the vessel connection to damp oscillations. The key principle — which is frequently missed — is that the orifice must be asymmetric:

  • Larger orifice for outflow (during down-surge — fast water delivery needed to prevent column separation)
  • Smaller orifice for inflow (during up-surge — controlled return to prevent slam and re-pressurisation spike)

7.4 Minimum water reserve

The vessel must retain a minimum water volume at all times to prevent gas entrainment into the pipeline. A low-level float switch with alarm and automatic refill valve is mandatory. The minimum water volume is typically set at 10–15% of total vessel volume.

7.5 Vessel location

The vessel is most effective when located within 50–100 m of the pump station discharge. The further the vessel, the longer the unprotected pipeline segment during the first critical milliseconds of the transient.

10. Conclusion

Surge vessel sizing is not a single-step calculation — it is an iterative design process that begins with an analytical estimate and is refined through transient simulation. The key lessons from this example:

  • The analytical method provided a starting point of 6.0 m³ — but simulation showed the actual required volume was 8.0 m³, a 33% increase.
  • The pre-charge pressure must reflect the actual HGL at the vessel tap point — errors here cascade through the entire calculation.
  • The connection pipe diameter directly affects vessel response speed and must be modelled explicitly in the simulation.
  • Always run the unprotected baseline first — it quantifies the risk and builds the case for the protection investment to the client.
  • The pressure-time trace at the pump discharge node is the single most important output for client reporting and design approval.
