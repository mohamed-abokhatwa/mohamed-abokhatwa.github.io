Sizing the Hydropneumatic Surge Vessel in Water Transmission Pipelines

By Mohamed Abokhatwa  ·  Technical Manager | PMP, PMI-RMP, ENV SP  ·  May 2026

Introduction

1. How the Surge Vessel Works

The surge vessel is a closed pressure vessel, partially filled with water and partially with compressed gas (nitrogen). When a pump trip occurs, two phases follow:

  • Phase 1 — Down-surge: Pipeline pressure drops. The vessel expels water into the pipeline to prevent column separation.
  • Phase 2 — Up-surge: The returning pressure wave compresses the gas cushion, absorbing the excess pressure rise.
The vessel does not eliminate the transient — it modulates it. The gas cushion acts as a spring: larger and softer means gentler modulation.

2. The Governing Equation — Polytropic Gas Law

The behaviour of the gas cushion is governed by:

P0 · V0^n = P1 · V1^n = constant
n = 1.2 adopted as standard for water transmission (Chaudhry, 2014; Thorley, 2004)

In practice n = 1.2 is the standard — between isothermal (n=1.0) and adiabatic (n=1.4). The water exchanged is simply: expelled = V1 - V0 and absorbed = V0 - V2.

The hydropneumatic surge vessel is one of the most effective devices for mitigating water hammer in water transmission pipelines. It acts as a local pressure reservoir — supplying water when pressure drops after a pump trip, and absorbing excess water when pressure rises.

Despite its critical role, surge vessel sizing is frequently oversimplified. In this article, I present a structured design workflow — from defining system inputs to interpreting Bentley HAMMER simulation outputs — using a real-scale pipeline example.

1. System Description and Design Scenario

The example system is a single pump station feeding a 12 km Ductile Iron transmission pipeline. The design scenario is a simultaneous trip of all operating pumps — the most critical transient event in most transmission systems.

3. Surge Vessel Sizing — Iterative Approach

1Analytical starting point — polytropic gas law

P₀ · V₀ⁿ = P₁ · V₁ⁿ  →  V₁ = V₀ × (P₀/P₁)^(1/n)
V₁ = 3.0 × (1.84/1.30)^(1/1.2) = 3.0 × 1.337 = 4.01 m³ → Initial vessel estimate: 6.0 m³
